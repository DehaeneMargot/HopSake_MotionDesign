How is Saké made?

HopSaké provides the finest of sakes, produced in Japan. Saké, just like wine and beer, is made through the fermentation of yeast. Unlike wine, Saké is made from rice, koji and water. From rice grain to bottle.

The rice grain

First, the husk, germ, nutrients, bran are removed (polishing). A carefully measured amount of rice is washed and soaked. After the washed and soaked rice is at the perfect condition for steaming, the rice is loaded into the rice steamer. The steaming allows the rice to maintain a firm outer texture and soft centre, thereby helping the brewing process.

The yeast

Koji making is a 48-hour process which involves the inoculation of rice with koji spores, careful kneading and control of temperature and humidity. Once the first batch of koji is ready, it is time to start mixing it into water and yeast in a fermentation tank, then adding steamed rice. The tank is filled gradually, in three stages over a 4-day period. This allows the yeast to retain its strength to keep consuming sugar and producing alcohol throughout the fermentation period, which typically continues for 21 days. The main mash is poured into a press, where it is separated into freshly pressed "new Saké".

The saké

First, the lees left in the Saké get removed. Pasteurization is the most important step that decides the quality. The Saké gets heated in a water-bath using the latest pasteurizers. After pasteurization, the Saké is placed in a maturing vat to mature. Finally, most Saké is diluted with water to lower the alcohol content from 18–20% down to 14–16%.

The bottle

Now that the Saké is ready to drink, it has to be bottled. Saké becomes more and more popular around the globe and gets shipped out from Japan. It then gets delivered right to your doorstep for you to discover and enjoy the world of Saké.
